Business Insights — Calculated Fields
1. Profit Margin
Formula: Profit divided by Sales

What it means: Profit Margin tells us what percentage of every sale is actually kept as profit after costs. In this dataset the overall profit margin is 15.3%. This means for every 100 units sold, the business keeps 15.3 as profit.

Why it is useful: It helps compare how profitable different categories, regions, or segments are — not just which ones sell the most. For example, Technology has an 18.2% margin while Furniture only has 6.9%. Even though Furniture generates a lot of sales, it is not very profitable. Without this field we would only see raw sales numbers and might wrongly assume Furniture is a good performer.

2. Cost
Formula: Sales minus Profit

What it means: Cost tells us how much was spent to generate each sale — covering things like product cost, shipping, and discounts. If a sale is 10,000 and profit is 1,500, the cost is 8,500.

Why it is useful: It helps identify where the business is spending the most to make a sale. High cost orders combined with low profit is a warning sign. It also helps management understand the cost structure behind each category or region without needing a separate cost dataset.

3. Average Order Value
Formula: Sales divided by Quantity

What it means: Average Order Value tells us how much revenue is generated per unit sold on average. A higher value means customers are buying more expensive items or larger quantities per transaction.

Why it is useful: It helps compare the quality of sales across segments and categories. For example, a Corporate customer buying Technology products likely has a much higher average order value than a Consumer buying Office Supplies. This helps leadership decide where to focus sales efforts for maximum revenue per transaction.

4. Return Rate
Formula: Returned orders divided by total orders

What it means: Return Rate tells us what percentage of orders are being sent back by customers. In this dataset the overall return rate is 4.5%. Furniture has the highest return rate at 7.7% while Technology is the lowest at 3.0%.

Why it is useful: Returns mean lost revenue and additional costs. A high return rate in a low-margin category like Furniture is particularly damaging because the business is already making little profit on those sales. Tracking return rate helps identify problem areas before they get worse.

5. Shipping Delay Bucket
Formula: Delivery days grouped into categories — Same Day, Fast (1-2 days), Standard (3-4 days), Slow (5-6 days), Very Slow (7+ days)

What it means: Instead of looking at raw delivery day numbers which are hard to read in a chart, this field groups orders into five clear delivery speed categories. For example an order that took 3 days goes into the Standard bucket.

Why it is useful: It makes it easy to see at a glance how quickly orders are being fulfilled across different shipping modes. It also helps spot whether slower delivery is linked to lower customer ratings or higher return rates — both of which would suggest delivery speed is affecting customer satisfaction.
